What is Intersectionality?

It is not a math problem (Race + Gender = Experience). Rather, intersectionality is the understanding that social identities (race, class, gender, sexuality) are inextricably linked.

Weber (1998) argues that we cannot understand race, class, gender, and sexuality in isolation. They are simultaneously operating systems of oppression and privilege, they are interdependent systems that define one another. Instead of asking "Which factor is more important?", we must ask "How do these factors rely on each other to maintain social hierarchies?" This web of connection forms the basis of our social reality.

Critical Intersections: Deep Dive

Gender, Class, & Residence

Economic class shapes how gender is "done." Wealthier families often have the resources to enact the "traditional" Breadwinner/Housewife model, whereas working-class families often require dual incomes, disrupting traditional gender roles out of necessity. Residence also plays a role; urban environments may offer more anonymity for gender non-conformity compared to tight-knit rural communities.

Gender & Race

Race fundamentally alters gender expectations. For example, Black men may face the hyper-masculinized "aggressive" stereotype, while Asian men are often desexualized or feminized in Western media. Similarly, Black women may not be afforded the same "fragility" or protection associated with white femininity, often dubbed the "Strong Black Woman" schema which can deny them care and support.

Sexual Orientation & Others

Sexual orientation intersects heavily with gender norms. For instance, a gay man may be seen as "failing" at masculinity. This intersects with race/class; in some communities, coming out risks not just social standing but family economic safety nets, leading to phenomena like the "Down Low" in specific subcultures where racial solidarity is prioritized over sexual identity.

Immigration Status

Immigration status creates a power imbalance often gendered. Women who depend on a spouse for legal visas (e.g., H4 visas) may be trapped in abusive dynamics, unable to leave without facing deportation. Men may face "downward mobility," unable to work in their field, challenging their traditional role as providers and impacting their gender performance.

Gendered Disability

Disability is often viewed through a gendered lens. Masculinity is often equated with physical strength and autonomy; thus, men with disabilities may feel a sharper loss of "manhood." Women with disabilities are often infantilized, assumed to be non-sexual, or unfit for motherhood, denying them traditional feminine milestones.

Age & Attractiveness

The "double standard" of aging is stark. Men often gain status as they age being refered to as a "silver fox," or being percieved as distinguished, while women are judged harshly for losing youth or "letting herself go". Attractiveness is a form of currency that women are taught to maintain, while men's worth is often tied more to economic output than physical beauty.
